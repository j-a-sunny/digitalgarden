## Part 1: Conceptual Foundation & Building Blocks

### 1. The Three Core Activities

Accounting is an information system that transforms raw economic data into useful financial stories through exactly three sequential activities:

* **Identification:** Selecting economic events relevant to a specific business entity (e.g., selling goods, paying rent). Non-economic events (e.g., hiring a new manager, answering a customer query) are **not** identified or recorded.
* **Recording:** Keeping a systematic, chronological diary of measured events. This involves classifying items (grouping like items together) and summarizing them.
* **Communication:** Preparing formal financial reports (financial statements) and analyzing/interpreting those reports using ratios and trends for users.

> **Distinction Note:** **Bookkeeping** is strictly limited to the mechanical *recording* activity of economic events. **Accounting** encompasses the entire process—identification, recording, analysis, and communication.

### 2. Information Users & Core Questions

* **Internal Users:** Managers who plan, organize, and run the business from within.
* *Marketing:* What price should we charge to maximize net income?
* *Finance:* Is there enough cash to pay dividends to stockholders?
* *Human Resources:* Can we afford employee pay raises this year?
* *Management:* Which product line is the most profitable?


* **External Users:** Individuals and organizations outside the entity.
* *Investors (Owners):* Is the company earning a satisfactory income? How does it compare in size and profitability with competitors?
* *Creditors (Suppliers/Banks):* Will the company be able to pay its debts when they come due?



### 3. The Regulatory Environment & Principles

* **Ethics:** The foundational standard of conduct by which actions are judged as right or wrong. Effective financial reporting is impossible without sound ethical behavior.
* **Standards Bodies:** * **FASB** (Financial Accounting Standards Board) develops **GAAP** (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles) used primarily in the US.
* **IASB** (International Accounting Standards Board) develops **IFRS** (International Financial Reporting Standards) used globally.


* **Measurement Principles:**
* **Historical Cost Principle:** Dictates that companies record assets at their *original purchase cost*. If you buy land for $50,000, it stays on your books at $50,000 even if its market value jumps to $100,000.
* **Fair Value Principle:** Dictates that assets and liabilities should be reported at fair value (current market price). *This is only used for situations where assets are actively traded, such as investment securities.*


* **Assumptions:**
* **Monetary Unit Assumption:** Requires that only transaction data capable of being expressed in terms of money be included in accounting records.
* **Economic Entity Assumption:** Requires that the activities of the entity be kept completely separate and distinct from the personal activities of its owner and any other business.


* **Forms of Business Ownership:**
* *Proprietorship:* Owned by one person; personally liable for business debts (owner's equity is categorized as Owner's Capital).
* *Partnership:* Owned by two or more partners; shared personal liability.
* *Corporation:* Organized as a separate legal entity under state law; ownership is divided into transferable shares of stock; owners enjoy limited liability.

## Part 2: The Mathematics of the Accounting Equation

The entire structural math of accounting relies on the basic equation, which must **always maintain strict mathematical balance**:

$$\text{Assets} = \text{Liabilities} + \text{Owner's Equity}$$

### The Expanded Accounting Equation

To solve algebraic math problems in Chapter 1, you must use the expanded version showing how operations alter equity:

$$\text{Assets} = \text{Liabilities} + \text{Owner's Capital} - \text{Owner's Drawings} + \text{Revenues} - \text{Expenses}$$

* **Assets:** Resources owned by the business that have future economic value (Cash, Accounts Receivable, Supplies, Prepaid Insurance, Equipment).
* **Liabilities:** Existing debts and obligations; claims of creditors against the assets (Accounts Payable, Notes Payable, Unearned Revenue, Salaries Payable).
* **Owner's Equity:** The residual claim of the owner on total assets ($\text{Assets} - \text{Liabilities}$).
* **Investments by Owner (+):** Assets put into the business by the owner.
* **Revenues (+):** Gross increases in equity resulting from business activities performed to earn income (Service Revenue, Sales Revenue).
* **Drawings (-):** Withdrawals of cash or other assets by the owner for personal use.
* **Expenses (-):** Cost of assets consumed or services used in the process of earning revenue (Rent Expense, Salaries Expense, Utilities Expense).

## Part 3: Deep-Dive Transaction Analysis (The Math)

Let’s mathematically map the classic 10 transactions of a startup business (e.g., "Softbyte") to see exactly how the equation balances after every step.

### Transaction 1: Initial Investment

*Marc Doucet invests $15,000 cash in a computer programming business.*

* **Math Effect:** Cash increases by $15,000. Owner’s Capital increases by $15,000.

$$\text{Cash }(+\$15,000) = \text{Owner's Capital }(+\$15,000)$$


$$\text{Balance: } \$15,000 = \$15,000$$



### Transaction 2: Purchase of Equipment for Cash

*Softbyte purchases computer equipment for $7,000 cash.*

* **Math Effect:** Cash decreases by $7,000. Equipment increases by $7,000.

$$\text{Cash }(-\$7,000) + \text{Equipment }(+\$7,000) = 0 \text{ (No change on right side)}$$


$$\text{Balance: } \underbrace{\text{Cash } \$8,000 + \text{Equipment } \$7,000}_{\$15,000} = \text{Capital } \$15,000$$



### Transaction 3: Purchase of Supplies on Credit

*Softbyte purchases computer paper and other supplies for $1,600 from Acme Supply Company on account.*

* **Math Effect:** Supplies (Asset) increases by $1,600. Accounts Payable (Liability) increases by $1,600.

$$\text{Supplies }(+\$1,600) = \text{Accounts Payable }(+\$1,600)$$


$$\text{Balance: } \underbrace{\text{Cash } \$8,000 + \text{Supplies } \$1,600 + \text{Equipment } \$7,000}_{\$16,600} = \underbrace{\text{Accounts Payable } \$1,600 + \text{Capital } \$15,000}_{\$16,600}$$



### Transaction 4: Services Provided for Cash

*Softbyte receives $1,200 cash from customers for programming services performed.*

* **Math Effect:** Cash increases by $1,200. Service Revenue increases by $1,200 (which increases Owner's Equity).

$$\text{Cash }(+\$1,200) = \text{Revenues }(+\$1,200)$$


$$\text{Balance: } \underbrace{\text{Cash } \$9,200 + \text{Supplies } \$1,600 + \text{Equipment } \$7,000}_{\$17,800} = \underbrace{\text{Accounts Payable } \$1,600 + \text{Capital } \$15,000 + \text{Revenues } \$1,200}_{\$17,800}$$



### Transaction 5: Purchase of Advertising on Credit

*Softbyte receives a bill for $250 from the Daily News for advertising but postpones payment until a later date.*

* **Math Effect:** Accounts Payable increases by $250. Advertising Expense increases by $250 (which decreases Owner's Equity).

$$0 = \text{Accounts Payable }(+\$250) - \text{Expenses }(+\$250)$$


$$\text{Balance: } \text{Assets } \$17,800 = \underbrace{\text{Accounts Payable } \$1,850 + \text{Capital } \$15,000 + \text{Rev } \$1,200 - \text{Exp } \$250}_{\$17,800}$$



### Transaction 6: Services Provided for Cash and Credit

*Softbyte performs programming services worth $3,500. The company receives $1,500 cash from customers, and bills the remaining $2,000 on account.*

* **Math Effect:** Cash increases by $1,500. Accounts Receivable (Asset) increases by $2,000. Service Revenue increases by $3,500.

$$\text{Cash }(+\$1,500) + \text{Accounts Receivable }(+\$2,000) = \text{Revenues }(+\$3,500)$$


$$\text{Balance: } \underbrace{\text{Cash } \$10,700 + \text{A/R } \$2,000 + \text{Supplies } \$1,600 + \text{Equip } \$7,000}_{\$21,300} = \underbrace{\text{A/P } \$1,850 + \text{Cap } \$15,000 + \text{Rev } \$4,700 - \text{Exp } \$250}_{\$21,300}$$



### Transaction 7: Payment of Expenses

*Softbyte pays the following expenses in cash for the month: store rent $600, salaries of employees $900, and utilities $200.*

* **Math Effect:** Cash decreases by $1,700 ($ \$600 + \$900 + \$200$). Expenses increase by $1,700 (reducing Owner's Equity).

$$\text{Cash }(-\$1,700) = -\text{Expenses }(+\$1,700)$$


$$\text{Balance: } \underbrace{\text{Cash } \$9,000 + \text{A/R } \$2,000 + \text{Supplies } \$1,600 + \text{Equip } \$7,000}_{\$19,600} = \underbrace{\text{A/P } \$1,850 + \text{Cap } \$15,000 + \text{Rev } \$4,700 - \text{Exp } \$1,950}_{\$19,600}$$



### Transaction 8: Payment of Accounts Payable

*Softbyte pays its $250 Daily News bill in cash.*

* **Math Effect:** Cash decreases by $250. Accounts Payable decreases by $250.

$$\text{Cash }(-\$250) = \text{Accounts Payable }(-\$250)$$


$$\text{Balance: } \underbrace{\text{Cash } \$8,750 + \text{A/R } \$2,000 + \text{Supplies } \$1,600 + \text{Equip } \$7,000}_{\$19,350} = \underbrace{\text{A/P } \$1,600 + \text{Cap } \$15,000 + \text{Rev } \$4,700 - \text{Exp } \$1,950}_{\$19,350}$$



### Transaction 9: Receipt of Cash on Account

*Softbyte receives $600 in cash from customers who had been billed in Transaction 6.*

* **Math Effect:** Cash increases by $600. Accounts Receivable decreases by $600. *Note: Revenue is NOT recorded here because it was already recorded back in transaction 6.*

$$\text{Cash }(+\$600) + \text{Accounts Receivable }(-\$600) = 0$$


$$\text{Balance: } \underbrace{\text{Cash } \$9,350 + \text{A/R } \$1,400 + \text{Supplies } \$1,600 + \text{Equip } \$7,000}_{\$19,350} = \underbrace{\text{A/P } \$1,600 + \text{Cap } \$15,000 + \text{Rev } \$4,700 - \text{Exp } \$1,950}_{\$19,350}$$



### Transaction 10: Withdrawal of Cash by Owner

*Marc Doucet withdraws $1,300 in cash from the business for personal use.*

* **Math Effect:** Cash decreases by $1,300. Owner’s Drawings increases by $1,300 (reducing Owner's Equity).

$$\text{Cash }(-\$1,300) = -\text{Drawings }(+\$1,300)$$


$$\text{Final Balance Math Summary:}$$


$$\text{Total Assets } = \$9,350 - \$1,300 + \$1,400 + \$1,600 + \$7,000 = \mathbf{\$18,050}$$


$$\text{Total Liabilities + Equity } = \$1,600 + \$15,000 - \$1,300 + \$4,700 - \$1,950 = \mathbf{\$18,050}$$

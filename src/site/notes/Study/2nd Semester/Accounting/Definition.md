### **Chapter 1: Accounting in Action Definitions**

- **Accounting:** An information system that **identifies, records, and communicates** the economic events of an organization to interested users.
    
- **Bookkeeping:** A part of the accounting process that involves only the mechanical **recording** of economic events.
    
- **Internal Users:** Managers who plan, organize, and run a business from within the organization (e.g., marketing managers, production supervisors, finance directors).
    
- **External Users:** Individuals and organizations outside a company who want financial information about the business (e.g., Investors, Creditors).
    
- **Investors (Owners):** External users who use accounting information to decide whether to buy, hold, or sell business stock.
    
- **Creditors (Suppliers/Bankers):** External users who evaluate the risks of granting credit or lending money to a business.
    
- **Ethics:** The standards of conduct by which one's actions are judged as right or wrong, honest or dishonest, fair or not fair.
    
- **Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP):** A common set of rules and practices, having substantial authoritative support, recognized by the accounting profession as a general guide for financial reporting purposes.
    
- **Historical Cost Principle (or Cost Principle):** An accounting rule dictating that companies must record assets at their **original purchase cost**.
    
- **Fair Value Principle:** An accounting principle stating that assets and liabilities should be reported at fair value (the market price received to sell an asset or settle a liability).
    
- **Monetary Unit Assumption:** An assumption requiring that companies include in the accounting records only transaction data that can be expressed in terms of money.
    
- **Economic Entity Assumption:** An assumption requiring that the activities of a business entity be kept separate and distinct from the activities of its owner and all other economic entities.
    
- **Proprietorship:** A business owned by one person, where the owner receives all profits, suffers all losses, and is personally liable for all business debts.
    
- **Partnership:** A business owned by two or more persons, typically characterized by retail or service-type operations and unlimited personal liability.
    
- **Corporation:** A business organized as a separate legal entity under state corporation law, with ownership divided into transferable shares of stock, giving owners limited liability.
    
- **Assets:** Resources owned by a business that provide future services or economic benefits (e.g., Cash, Supplies, Equipment).
    
- **Liabilities:** Existing claims against assets representing a company's debts and obligations to creditors.
    
- **Owner’s Equity:** The ownership claim on total assets, often referred to as residual equity ($\text{Assets} - \text{Liabilities}$).
    
- **Investments by Owner:** The assets (such as cash) that an owner puts into the business.
    
- **Revenues:** Gross increases in owner's equity resulting from business activities entered into for the purpose of earning income (e.g., sales, fees, services).
    
- **Drawings:** Decreases in owner's equity caused when an owner withdraws cash or other assets for personal use.
    
- **Expenses:** The cost of assets consumed or services used in the process of earning revenue (e.g., salaries expense, rent expense).
    
- **Transactions:** A business's economic events recorded by accountants that alter the financial position (assets, liabilities, or owner's equity) of a company.
    
- **Income Statement:** A financial report that presents the revenues and expenses of a company to show its net income or net loss for a specific period of time.
    
- **Net Income:** Earned during a time period when total revenues exceed total expenses ($\text{Revenues} > \text{Expenses}$).
    
- **Net Loss:** Incurred during a time period when total expenses exceed total revenues ($\text{Expenses} > \text{Revenues}$).
    
- **Owner’s Equity Statement:** A financial statement that summarizes the changes in owner’s equity for a specific period of time.
    
- **Balance Sheet:** A financial statement acting as a "snapshot" that reports the assets, liabilities, and owner’s equity of a company at a specific date.
    
- **Statement of Cash Flows:** A financial statement providing information about a specific period's cash receipts (inflows) and cash payments (outflows).
    
- **Forensic Accounting:** An accounting field that uses accounting, auditing, and investigative skills to conduct investigations into theft and fraud.
    

### **Chapter 2: The Recording Process Definitions**

- **Account:** An individual accounting record of increases and decreases in a specific asset, liability, owner's equity, revenue, or expense item.
    
- **T-Account:** The simplest visual form of an account, structured like a "T", consisting of a title, a left side (Debit), and a right side (Credit).
    
- **Debit (Dr.):** The **left** side of an accounting T-account.
    
- **Credit (Cr.):** The **right** side of an accounting T-account.
    
- **Double-Entry System:** A foundational system where each business transaction affects **two or more accounts** to ensure total debits equal total credits and keep the equation balanced.
    
- **Normal Balance:** The side of an account (Debit or Credit) where an **increase** is recorded under standard accounting rules.
    
- **Journal:** A chronological record of transactions, referred to as the **book of original entry**, showing complete debit and credit effects in one place.
    
- **Journalizing:** The mechanical process of entering transaction data chronologically into the journal.
    
- **Compound Journal Entry:** A journal entry that involves three or more individual accounts (e.g., debiting one account and crediting two).
    
- **General Ledger:** A collection or book containing the entire group of individual asset, liability, owner's equity, revenue, and expense accounts maintained by a company.
    
- **Posting:** The process of transferring chronological journal entry information into individual account categories within the ledger.
    
- **Chart of Accounts:** A systematic list of all the account titles used by a business, usually ordered by assets, liabilities, equity, revenues, and expenses.
    
- **Trial Balance:** A compiled list of accounts and their ledger balances at a specific point in time, used primarily to prove the mathematical equality of total debits and credits.
    

### **Quick Formula & Rule Reference for the Test**

$$\text{Assets} = \text{Liabilities} + \text{Owner's Capital} - \text{Owner's Drawings} + \text{Revenues} - \text{Expenses}$$

**Normal Balance Rule (The "DEAD" Mnemonic):**

- **D**ebit increases **E**xpenses, **A**ssets, and **D**rawings. (Normal Balance = **Debit**).
    
- **Credit** increases Liabilities, Capital, and Revenues. (Normal Balance = **Credit**).

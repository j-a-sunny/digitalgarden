## Part 1: The Account and T-Account Mechanics

An **account** is an individual accounting record of increases and decreases in a specific asset, liability, owner's equity, revenue, or expense item.

In its simplest format, an account consists of three parts arranged like the letter **T** (hence the term **T-Account**):

1. **The Title** (e.g., Cash, Accounts Payable, Service Revenue).
    
2. A left side called **Debit (Dr.)**.
    
3. A right side called **Credit (Cr.)**.
    

### The Fundamental Rule of Debits and Credits

- **Debiting** an account means entering an amount on the **left** side.
    
- **Crediting** an account means entering an amount on the **right** side.
    
- **Balancing an Account:** You add up the total debits and total credits. If total debits exceed total credits, the account has a **debit balance**. If total credits exceed total debits, it has a **credit balance**.
    

## Part 2: The Double-Entry System & Normal Balances

Under the **double-entry system**, every business transaction must affect **two or more accounts** to keep the basic accounting equation in balance. Furthermore, for every transaction:

$$\text{Total Debits} = \text{Total Credits}$$

### The Debit/Credit Matrix (Normal Balances)

An account's **Normal Balance** is the side (Debit or Credit) where an increase is recorded.

Here is the exact mathematical behavior for each account category:

| **Account Category** | **Increase Side** | **Decrease Side** | **Normal Balance** |
| -------------------- | ----------------- | ----------------- | ------------------ |
| **Assets**           | Debit ($+$)       | Credit ($-$)      | **Debit**          |
| **Liabilities**      | Credit ($+$)      | Debit ($-$)       | **Credit**         |
| **Owner's Capital**  | Credit ($+$)      | Debit ($-$)       | **Credit**         |
| **Owner's Drawings** | Debit ($+$)       | Credit ($-$)      | **Debit**          |
| **Revenues**         | Credit ($+$)      | Debit ($-$)       | **Credit**         |
| **Expenses**         | Debit ($+$)       | Credit ($-$)      | **Debit**          |

> **Crucial Exam Mnemonic:** Remember **DEAD**
> 
> - **D**ebit increases **E**xpenses, **A**ssets, and **D**rawings. (These all have a normal **Debit** balance).
>     
> - Consequently, Liabilities, Capital, and Revenues are increased by **Credits** (and have a normal **Credit** balance).
>     

## Part 3: Steps in the Recording Process

The accounting recording process does not happen directly in the ledger. It follows three precise, linear steps:

$$\text{1. Analyze Business Transactions} \longrightarrow \text{2. Enter in Journal} \longrightarrow \text{3. Post to Ledger}$$

### Step 1: Analyze Transactions

Determine what accounts are affected, whether they increase or decrease, and whether they should be debited or credited using source documents (sales slips, checks, bills).

### Step 2: Journalize (The Journal)

Transactions are recorded chronologically in a **Journal** (often called the _book of original entry_).

- **Why it's important:** It prevents errors because it shows the complete debit and credit effects of a transaction in one place, provides a chronological record, and helps locate errors.
    
- **Journal Entry Formatting Rules for the Exam:**
    
    1. The date is entered in the date column.
        
    2. The account to be **debited** is entered first next to the margin, with its amount in the left column.
        
    3. The account to be **credited** is listed on the next line, **indented** to the right, with its amount in the right column.
        
    4. A brief explanation is written directly below the entry.
        

#### Standard vs. Compound Journal Entries

- **Standard Entry:** Involves exactly one debit account and one credit account.
    
- **Compound Entry:** Involves three or more accounts (e.g., buying a $25,000 asset by paying $10,000 cash and signing a $15,000 note). The mathematical rule still stands: **Total Debits must equal Total Credits**.
    

### Step 3: Posting (The Ledger)(খতিয়ান)

The **General Ledger** contains all the asset, liability, owner's equity, revenue, and expense accounts maintained by a company.

- **Posting** is the administrative process of transferring journal entries from the chronological journal into their individual respective accounts inside the general ledger.
    

## Part 4: Comprehensive Recording Process Math

Let’s apply these rules to a full-cycle mathematical practice problem.

**Scenario:** Bob opens Campus Laundromat on January 1. Let's record his transactions, see how they form journal entries, post them, and track balances.

### Transaction 1 (Jan 1): Owner Investment

_Bob invests Tk. 20,000 cash in the business._

- **Analysis:** Cash (Asset) increases $\rightarrow$ Debit. Owner's Capital (Equity) increases $\rightarrow$ Credit.
    
- **Journal Entry:**
    
    Plaintext
    
    ```
    Jan 1   Cash .................................. 20,000
                Bob, Capital ............................. 20,000
            (To record investment of cash by owner)
    ```
    

### Transaction 2 (Jan 2): Payment of Rent

_The company paid Tk. 1,000 cash for store rent for January._

- **Analysis:** Rent Expense (Expense) increases $\rightarrow$ Debit. Cash (Asset) decreases $\rightarrow$ Credit.
    
- **Journal Entry:**
    
    Plaintext
    
    ```
    Jan 2   Rent Expense ..........................  1,000
                Cash .....................................  1,000
            (To record payment of rent for January)
    ```
    

### Transaction 3 (Jan 3): Compound Purchase of Equipment

_Purchased washers and dryers for Tk. 25,000, paying Tk. 10,000 in cash and signing a Tk. 15,000, 6-month, 12% note payable._

- **Analysis:** Equipment (Asset) increases $\rightarrow$ Debit Tk. 25,000. Cash (Asset) decreases $\rightarrow$ Credit Tk. 10,000. Notes Payable (Liability) increases $\rightarrow$ Credit Tk. 15,000.
    
- **Journal Entry (Compound):**
    
    Plaintext
    
    ```
    Jan 3   Equipment ............................. 25,000
                Cash ..................................... 10,000
                Notes Payable ............................ 15,000
            (To record purchase of laundry equipment)
    ```
    
    _Math Check:_ $\text{Debits } (25,000) = \text{Credits } (10,000 + 15,000 = 25,000)$.
    

### Transaction 4 (Jan 4): Purchase of Insurance

_Paid Tk. 1,200 for a one-year accident insurance policy._

- **Analysis:** Prepaid Insurance (Asset) increases $\rightarrow$ Debit. Cash (Asset) decreases $\rightarrow$ Credit.
    
- **Journal Entry:**
    
    Plaintext
    
    ```
    Jan 4   Prepaid Insurance ........................  1,200
    	    Cash .....................................  1,200
           (To record payment of 1-year policy)
    ```
    

## Part 5: The Trial Balance (রেওামিল)

A **Trial Balance** is a list of accounts and their ledger balances at a specific point in time (usually the end of the month).

### How to Construct It

Accounts are listed in order of their appearance in the ledger: **Assets, Liabilities, Owner's Equity, Revenues, and Expenses**. Their balances are placed in either the Debit or Credit column based on their normal balances.

Let's compute the ledger balances for **Cash** from our problem above to see how it transfers:

- _Jan 1:_ Debit of 20,000
    
- _Jan 2:_ Credit of 1,000
    
- _Jan 3:_ Credit of 10,000
    
- _Jan 4:_ Credit of 1,200
    
- $$\text{Cash Ledger Balance} = 20,000 - 1,000 - 10,000 - 1,200 = \mathbf{7,800 \text{ (Debit Balance)}}$$
    

### Example Trial Balance Compilation Math

Plaintext

```
Campus Laundromat
Trial Balance
January 31

Accounts                    Debit (Tk.)     Credit (Tk.)
--------------------------------------------------------
Cash                           7,800
Prepaid Insurance              1,200
Equipment                     25,000
Notes Payable                                   15,000
Bob, Capital                                    20,000
Rent Expense                   1,000
--------------------------------------------------------
TOTALS                        35,000            35,000
```

_The mathematical check is successful: $\text{Total Debits (35,000)} = \text{Total Credits (35,000)}$._

### Crucial Exam Topic: Limitations of a Trial Balance

Your exam will almost certainly ask you this theoretical trick question: **Does an equal trial balance mean no errors were made? No!** A trial balance will still balance perfectly even if:

1. A transaction is **completely omitted** from the system.
    
2. A correct journal entry is **never posted** to the ledger.
    
3. A journal entry is **posted twice**.
    
4. A transaction is recorded in the **wrong account** but with the correct amount (e.g., debiting Supplies instead of Equipment).
    
5. **Offsetting errors** are made (e.g., entering an extra Tk. 500 on both the debit side and credit side by mistake).
    

_Note: It will only show an error if a number is modified unequally (e.g., debiting an account for Tk. 1,000 but crediting the other for Tk. 100)._

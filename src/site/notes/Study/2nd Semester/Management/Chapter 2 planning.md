# CHAPTER 2: PLANNING & DECISION MAKING (PART 1)

## 1. Defining Planning

In management theory, **Planning** is the primary managerial function that determines in advance what should be done, how it should be done, when it should be done, and who should do it. It establishes a rational bridge from where the organization currently stands to where it aspires to be in the future.

- **According to Koontz & O'Donnell:** _"Planning is deciding in advance what to do, how to do it, when to do it, and who is to do it. Planning bridges the gap from where we are to where we want to go."_
    
- **The Core Focus:** It minimizes guesswork, reduces uncertainty, and ensures that organizational actions are purposeful rather than reactive.
    

## 2. Nature and Characteristics of Planning

To fully understand the role of planning within an enterprise, it is broken down into its fundamental characteristics:

1. **Primary Function (Primacy of Planning):** Planning is the foundational block of management. It precedes all other managerial functions—Organizing, Staffing, Directing, and Controlling. An executive cannot structure departments or assign tasks until the plan explicitly sets the objective.
    
2. **Goal-Oriented:** Planning has no independent meaning unless it serves to achieve specific corporate targets. Every plan must lay out an actionable pathway toward a defined purpose.
    
3. **Pervasiveness:** Planning is required at all levels of management. It is not exclusively a top-executive task. While the CEO creates long-term strategic corporate plans, a department manager designs tactical monthly schedules, and a frontline supervisor creates daily operational shifts.
    
4. **Continuous Process:** A plan is never permanently static. Because the internal and external environments are constantly shifting, planning is an ongoing cycle of formulation, execution, appraisal, and revision.
    
5. **Futuristic (Forward-Looking):** Planning is essentially looking ahead. It involves forecasting future events, customer demands, market trends, and technological disruptions to prepare the organization today.
    
6. **Intellectual and Rational Process:** It is a mental exercise requiring deliberate choice, critical thinking, creative imagination, and data-driven calculations. It replaces impulsive "rule-of-thumb" decisions with structured logic.
    
7. **Efficiency and Optimization:** The ultimate yardstick of a good plan is whether it maximizes organizational output while minimizing total resource costs (time, money, materials, and human labor).
    

## 3. Importance of Planning

Without a structured plan, an organization drifts aimlessly. The critical advantages of planning include:

- **Provides Direction:** By detailing explicit targets, it ensures that every department and employee knows exactly what goals they are working toward, unifying their efforts.
    
- **Reduces the Impact of Uncertainty:** While planning cannot eliminate future economic or market changes, it allows managers to anticipate them, perform scenario modeling, and build proactive contingency strategies.
    
- **Minimizes Overlapping and Wasteful Activities:** By coordinating activities beforehand, planning prevents chaotic work conditions where departments accidentally duplicate tasks or work at cross-purposes.
    
- **Promotes Innovation and Creativity:** Since planning forces managers to look for the "one best way" to achieve goals, it demands out-of-the-box thinking, which fosters organizational innovation.
    
- **Facilitates Control:** Planning establishes the performance metrics and standards. Without planned goals, a manager has no objective benchmarks to measure, compare, and control actual employee output.
    
- **Improves Resource Allocation:** It ensures that capital, raw materials, and staff hours are accurately assigned to high-priority zones, eliminating operational resource blockages.
    

## 4. The Complete 8-Step Planning Process

When creating an official plan, managers follow a strict, logical sequence of steps:

### Step 1: Establishment of Objectives

The initial step is to define clear, specific, and measurable goals for the entire organization, which are then broken down down into departmental and individual tasks. These objectives provide the endpoint milestones for all business efforts and should ideally follow the **SMART** criteria (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound).

### Step 2: Establishment of Planning Premises

Planning premises are the assumptions, forecasts, and anticipated conditions regarding the future environment in which the plan will operate. Because the future is uncertain, managers must establish these baseline boundaries:

- **Internal Premises:** Controllable internal factors (e.g., cash flow availability, existing manufacturing capacities, technological infrastructure, and human skills).
    
- **External Premises:** Uncontrollable macro-environmental factors (e.g., changes in government tax laws, inflation rates, socio-cultural consumer shifts, and competitive rival actions).
    

### Step 3: Identification of Alternative Courses of Action

Rarely is there only one way to achieve an organizational goal. In this phase, managers brainstorm, discover, and list all possible strategic routes and operational methods that could lead to the established objectives.

### Step 4: Evaluation of Alternatives

Once a pool of alternatives is identified, each option is thoroughly evaluated against critical organizational constraints:

- **Cost-Benefit Analysis:** What is the financial expenditure versus the projected return?
    
- **Feasibility:** Do we possess the technical machinery and staff talent to execute this option?
    
- **Risk Profile:** What are the potential vulnerabilities or legal complications associated with this path?
    

### Step 5: Selecting the Best Alternative

This is the formal point of decision-making. Based on the evaluation step, the manager selects the single most optimal plan (or a hybrid combination of multiple choices) that offers the highest probability of success with the lowest relative risk.

### Step 6: Formulation of Derivative (Supporting) Plans

A master plan cannot stand alone. To give it operational traction, departmental heads must create supporting, secondary plans. For example, a master corporate plan to launch a new product line requires derivative plans such as a raw material purchasing plan, an HR hiring plan, a promotional marketing plan, and a localized distribution schedule.

### Step 7: Securing Cooperation and Communication

Plans can fail if the people executing them do not understand or accept them. Management must systematically communicate the finalized blueprints down the line of command to explain the "why" behind the strategy, assign precise accountability, and gather employee buy-in.

### Step 8: Follow-up and Appraisal (The Feedback Loop)

As the plan goes live, managers must continuously track, evaluate, and monitor real-world performance against the original benchmarks. If the external planning premises shift (e.g., a sudden economic recession or rival price cut), the feedback loop allows managers to pivot and modify the operational plans dynamically.

## 1. Comprehensive Classification of Plans (The Hierarchy)

In management, plans are not uniform. To prevent organizational chaos, they are systematically grouped using three distinct classification baselines: frequency of use, time frames, and organizational scope.

### A. Based on Time Horizon (Timeframes)

1. **Long-Term Plans (Strategic):** * **Duration:** Typically covers a horizon of **5 to 10+ years**.
    
    - **Characteristics:** Highly visionary, broad, and abstract. Created exclusively by top executive management (Board, CEO). They handle macro issues like global market expansion, corporate restructuring, or entering completely new lines of business.
        
2. **Medium-Term Plans (Tactical):** * **Duration:** Spans a timeline of **1 to 5 years**.
    
    - **Characteristics:** Designed to connect long-term visions with practical daily activities. Formulated by middle-level managers (Divisional Heads, Department Managers) to outline how specific business units will allocate resources to support corporate targets.
        
3. **Short-Term Plans (Operational):** * **Duration:** Designed for a period of **less than 1 year** (down to months, weeks, or days).
    
    - **Characteristics:** Highly detailed, concrete, and specific. Drafted by lower-level supervisors to guide daily routines and ensure equipment, materials, and labor forces run optimally.
        

### B. Based on Organizational Scope (Functional Areas)

- **Production Plans:** Lay out raw inventory processing requirements, assembly plant floor capacities, and quality control test parameters.
    
- **Marketing Plans:** Focus on brand positioning, consumer acquisition campaigns, and market share percentage increases.
    
- **Financial Plans:** Detail capital structure ratios, incoming cash flows, dividend repayments, and asset investment distributions.
    
- **Human Resource Plans:** Track upcoming retirement gaps, temporary shift shortages, skill-set training schedules, and active recruitment pipelines.
    

## 2. Classification Based on Frequency of Use

This is one of the most critical structural divisions in planning theory. Plans are divided into **Standing Plans** (reusable roadmaps for routine work) and **Single-Use Plans** (custom roadmaps for specific events).

### Category I: Standing Plans (Ongoing/Continuous Guidelines)

Standing plans provide a uniform organizational response to repetitive, everyday situations. They instill structural consistency across the enterprise.

#### 1. Policies

- **Definition:** Broad, general statements or understandings that establish boundaries and provide direction for managerial decision-making.
    
- **Nature:** Policies do not force a single final action; instead, they grant an acceptable area of administrative discretion.
    
- **Example:** _"Our company policy is to prioritize internal employee promotion before seeking outside market talent."_ This guides the HR head but does not name a specific person.
    

#### 2. Procedures

- **Definition:** A rigid, chronological sequence of specific, interlinked steps required to execute a routine activity.
    
- **Nature:** Procedures cut across departmental boundaries and eliminate independent discretion to ensure tasks are completed in an identical sequence every single time.
    
- **Example:** The procedure for processing customer refunds: (1) Accept return form $\rightarrow$ (2) Verify original product condition $\rightarrow$ (3) Issue accounting system credit voucher $\rightarrow$ (4) Email confirmation code to the customer.
    

#### 3. Methods

- **Definition:** A detailed manual or technical guideline showing exactly how a single individual step within a larger procedure must be executed.
    
- **Nature:** Highly specific and focused on optimizing individual task efficiency.
    
- **Example:** Within step 2 of the refund procedure above (verifying product condition), the _method_ specifies using a particular barcode scanning tool and checking specific safety seals.
    

#### 4. Rules

- **Definition:** Explicit, absolute commands that dictate exactly what must or must not be done in a specific situation.
    
- **Nature:** Rules leave **zero room for interpretation or individual choice**. They are completely inflexible and carry strict penalties for non-compliance.
    
- **Example:** _"No smoking or open flames allowed inside the fuel repository compound."_ Or, _"All workers must wear protective headgear on the construction site floor."_
    

### Category II: Single-Use Plans (Non-Recurring Frameworks)

Single-use plans are custom-tailored to handle unique, non-repetitive business situations. Once the underlying project or timeframe is completed, these plans become obsolete.

#### 1. Objectives / Goals

- **Definition:** The fundamental endpoints or future targets an organization explicitly designs its operations to reach.
    
- **Nature:** They act as the destination markers for all corporate effort. They are single-use in the sense that once a specific 2026 target is hit, a new, distinct target is set for 2027.
    

#### 2. Strategies

- **Definition:** The comprehensive, long-term pattern of moves and resource deployments used to position an enterprise advantageously against its global competitive marketplace.
    
- **Nature:** Formulated in direct response to competitor behaviors and shifting consumer trends.
    

#### 3. Programs

- **Definition:** A large-scale, complex matrix of goals, individual policies, precise procedures, isolated rules, task assignments, and necessary financial budgets combined to execute a major, one-time corporate project.
    
- **Nature:** Highly resource-intensive and tracking multiple sub-moving parts over an extended period.
    
- **Example:** A program to construct and transition the enterprise to a new $50 million automated shipping facility.
    

#### 4. Budgets

- **Definition:** A statement of anticipated operational or financial results expressed entirely in clear, numerical parameters for a defined upcoming future period.
    
- **Nature:** Acts as both a planning tool (allocating scarce cash) and a control tool (tracking variances between estimated costs and actual cash spent).
    
- **Example:** The 2026 Q3 Marketing Department Budget: allocated exactly $\text{Tk. } 15,00,000$ for digital ads and $\text{Tk. } 5,00,000$ for printing.
    

## 3. The Planning Environment (SWOT & PESTEL Foundations)

Managers never plan in a vacuum. A plan must perfectly map against the internal realities of the business and the external trends of the wider world.

### A. The Internal Planning Environment (Highly Controllable)

These factors are contained within the borders of the enterprise. Management has direct authority to adjust, improve, or downsize these elements:

- **Organizational Culture:** The shared behavioral expectations, ethical norms, and company values that define employee interactions.
    
- **Financial Capital Availability:** Liquid cash reserves, lines of credit, and retained corporate earnings available to fund new strategic initiatives.
    
- **Physical Assets & Infrastructure:** The technological modernity of factory machinery, computer systems, and corporate facilities.
    
- **Human Capital Competency:** The specialized technical skills, baseline productivity rates, and management expertise of the active workforce.
    

### B. The External Planning Environment (Completely Uncontrollable)

These are macroeconomic and societal forces operating completely outside the direct influence of the company. A plan must accurately anticipate these elements to survive:

- **Economic Forces:** Shifting inflation figures, national interest rates, currency conversion volatility, and consumer disposable income indices.
    
- **Political & Legal Forces:** Sudden adjustments to corporate taxation structures, import-export trade barriers, and state labor welfare mandates.
    
- **Socio-Cultural Forces:** Shifting population demographics, generational lifestyle changes, and consumer preference movements.
    
- **Technological Forces:** The blistering pace of digital breakthroughs, automation updates, and platform obsolescence.
    
- **Competitive Forces:** Aggressive price changes, product imitation campaigns, and marketing strategies deployed by business rivals.
    
## 1. The Context of Decision-Making in Management

Decision-making is the core mechanism of management. Every planning choice requires identifying an issue or opportunity and selecting an optimal solution from a group of choices.

### The Rational Decision-Making Process

To avoid subjective or emotional choices, managers rely on a systematic, 7-step rational model:

1. **Identification of a Problem:** Recognizing an operational variance between an actual state of affairs (e.g., sales dropping by 15%) and the desired benchmark target.
    
2. **Identification of Decision Criteria:** Determining what factors are relevant to resolving the problem (e.g., budget limits, implementation speed, safety regulations, or required technology).
    
3. **Allocation of Weights to Criteria:** Prioritizing the identified criteria by assigning numerical weights to indicate their relative importance to the business.
    
4. **Development of Alternatives:** Brainstorming, researching, and listing viable solutions that could address the core issue.
    
5. **Analysis of Alternatives:** Scrutinizing each alternative against the weighted criteria to calculate an objective score for every option.
    
6. **Selection and Implementation of an Alternative:** Choosing the highest-scoring path, allocating resources, and communicating assignments to put the decision into effect.
    
7. **Evaluation of Decision Effectiveness:** Assessing whether the problem was resolved. If the problem persists, the manager steps back to analyze where the process failed.
    

## 2. Decision-Making Conditions (Certainty, Risk, and Uncertainty)

A manager's ability to make choices depends heavily on the amount of information available about the future outcomes of their actions:

- **Certainty:** An ideal situation where a manager can make highly accurate choices because the exact outcome of every single alternative is known in advance (e.g., investing in fixed government bonds).
    
- **Risk:** A common situation where a manager can estimate the likelihood or probability of certain outcomes based on historical records, industry data, or secondary research (e.g., launching a retail store in a familiar location).
    
- **Uncertainty:** A difficult situation where a manager faces a unique problem with no historical data, making it impossible to assign reasonable probabilities to the outcomes of their choices (e.g., launching an unproven technological innovation during an economic crisis).
    

## 3. Systematic Types of Management Decisions

Decisions are classified into distinct structural groups based on their frequency and their place in the organizational hierarchy:

### A. Programmed vs. Non-Programmed Decisions

- **Programmed Decisions:** Repetitive, structured choices that occur frequently enough for an organization to establish specific rules, policies, or procedures to handle them automatically (e.g., processing a standard customer return or reordering office supplies).
    
- **Non-Programmed Decisions:** Unique, poorly structured choices that are non-recurring and require custom-tailored solutions, critical thinking, and substantial resource analysis (e.g., deciding whether to acquire a competing firm).
    

### B. Hierarchical Decisions

- **Strategic Decisions:** High-stakes, long-term choices made by top executives that determine the overall direction, vision, and market positioning of the entire enterprise.
    
- **Tactical Decisions:** Medium-term choices made by middle management to coordinate resource distribution and implement strategic targets within specific business units.
    
- **Operational Decisions:** Day-to-day choices made by frontline supervisors to ensure immediate, short-term workflows run smoothly.
    

## 4. Management by Objectives (MBO)

**Management by Objectives (MBO)** is a collaborative management system where supervisors and subordinates meet to jointly determine clear goals, align individual tasks with organizational plans, and use those precise metrics to evaluate employee performance.

### The 5-Step MBO Cycle

1. **Formulating Overall Organizational Objectives:** Top-level executives define the company's master goals for the upcoming year or quarter.
    
2. **Setting Subordinate Objectives:** Departmental heads sit down one-on-one with their employees to collaboratively establish aligned, individual performance targets.
    
3. **Monitoring Progress:** Continuous tracking systems are maintained to check day-to-day work milestones against the agreed-upon criteria.
    
4. **Providing Performance Feedback:** Regular, scheduled performance reviews are conducted to discuss challenges and make necessary operational adjustments.
    
5. **Performance Evaluation and Rewards:** At the end of the time horizon, the subordinate's actual achievements are formally appraised against the joint goals established in Step 2. Successful outcomes are tied directly to promotions, bonuses, or career advancements.

## Summery

### I. Core Philosophy of Planning

- **Definition:** Deciding in advance the _what, how, when,_ and _who_ of future organizational action. It bridges the gap between where an enterprise currently stands and where it wants to be.
    
- **Core Characteristics:** It is the **primary** foundational function of management, **all-pervasive** (needed at all levels), **continuous** (constantly revised), **futuristic**, and a highly **intellectual process** driven by logic rather than instinct.
    

### II. The 8-Step Planning Process

1. **Establish Objectives:** Define clear, SMART organizational endpoints.
    
2. **Develop Premises:** Formulate core baseline assumptions about the internal (controllable) and external (uncontrollable) future environments.
    
3. **Identify Alternatives:** Brainstorm multiple paths to achieve the target.
    
4. **Evaluate Alternatives:** Weigh each path based on costs, risks, and feasibility constraints.
    
5. **Select the Best Course:** Make the formal decision to adopt the optimal strategy or hybrid mix.
    
6. **Formulate Derivative Plans:** Create supporting departmental actions (e.g., purchasing, marketing, hiring).
    
7. **Secure Cooperation:** Unify the line of command through active communication.
    
8. **Follow-up & Review:** Establish a feedback loop to monitor real-world execution against benchmarks.
    

### III. The Matrix of Plans

Plans are structurally divided by their frequency of use:

- **Standing Plans (Reusable Guidelines):** * _Policies:_ Broad guidelines allowing management discretion (e.g., internal promotion preference).
    
    - _Procedures:_ Sequential, chronological steps to handle routine activities (e.g., processing refunds).
        
    - _Methods:_ Highly detailed technical instructions for a single step within a procedure.
        
    - _Rules:_ Absolute commands with zero employee discretion (e.g., "No Smoking").
        
- **Single-Use Plans (Unique/Non-Recurring):** * _Objectives:_ The targeted results to be achieved.
    
    - _Strategies:_ High-level competitive patterns of response to the marketplace.
        
    - _Programs:_ Large-scale, complex mixes of goals, policies, and budgets for specific projects.
        
    - _Budgets:_ Numerical statements projecting operational, financial, or physical resource expectations.
        

### IV. Rational Decision-Making

- **Definition:** The analytical act of choosing the single best strategy from a pool of alternatives to solve an organizational variance.
    
- **The 7-Step Sequence:** Identify Problem $\rightarrow$ Identify Decision Criteria $\rightarrow$ Allocate Weights $\rightarrow$ Develop Alternatives $\rightarrow$ Analyze Options $\rightarrow$ Select & Implement $\rightarrow$ Evaluate Effectiveness.
    
- **Conditions:** Decisions are made under states of **Certainty** (perfect information), **Risk** (estimable probabilities), or **Uncertainty** (zero historical data).
    
- **Types:** Classed into routine **Programmed Decisions** (handled via standing rules) vs. unique **Non-Programmed Decisions** (requiring custom executive analysis).
    

### V. Management by Objectives (MBO)

- **Definition:** A collaborative managerial technique emphasizing joint target-setting.
    
- **The MBO Cycle:** Establish Corporate Goals $\rightarrow$ Jointly Set Subordinate Objectives $\rightarrow$ Continuously Monitor Progress $\rightarrow$ Provide Regular Performance Feedback $\rightarrow$ Appraise Final Performance & Allocate Rewards.

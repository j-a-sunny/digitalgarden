## 1. Scientific Management
Scientific management is defined as **the use of the scientific method to determine the "one best way" for a job to be done**.
 * **The Father of Scientific Management:** Frederick Winslow Taylor (1856–1915). Working at Midvale Steel Company, Taylor observed widespread inefficiencies. He sought to trigger a "mental revolution" among both workers and managers by defining clear guidelines to optimize production efficiency.
### Taylor's Four Principles of Scientific Management
 1. **Develop a Science for Each Element of Work:** Replace the old, inefficient "rule of thumb" methods with scientifically determined work methods.
 2. **Scientific Selection and Training:** Scientifically select workers and then train, teach, and develop them (rather than letting them choose their own work and train themselves).
 3. **Hearty Cooperation:** Cooperate closely with workers to ensure all work is being done in accordance with the scientific principles developed.
 4. **Division of Work and Responsibility:** Divide work and responsibility almost equally between management and workers. Management takes over all work for which it is better suited than the workers (e.g., planning and designing tasks).
## 2. General Administrative Theory
General administrative theorists are writers who developed broad, comprehensive theories concerning what managers do and what constitutes good management practice. The two primary figures highlighted are **Henri Fayol** and **Max Weber**.
 * **The Father of Modern Management:** Henri Fayol. He recognized management as a universal set of functions—**planning, organizing, leading, and controlling**—applicable to all types of organizations. While Taylor focused on first-line managers and shop-floor efficiency, Fayol focused on the activities of all managers and broader organizational structure.
## 3. Fayol's Fourteen Principles of Management
Fayol proposed 14 essential principles to guide effective organizational management:
 1. **Division of Work:** Specialization and deduction of the number of objectives towards which attention and effort are directed to achieve homogeneity and efficiency.
 2. **Authority and Responsibility:** Authority is the legitimate power to give orders and influence others. Responsibility is the natural corollary (result) of authority; if you apply authority, you are held accountable.
 3. **Discipline:** Every organization has its own rules and regulations. All employees, from "sweeper to owner," must respect and abide by them.
 4. **Unity of Command:** Every employee should receive orders from **only one** superior to avoid conflict and confusion.
 5. **Unity of Direction:** The organization should have a single plan of action to guide managers and workers. Departmental objectives must align seamlessly with the main goals of the enterprise.
 6. **Subordination of Individual Interest to General Interest:** The interests of any single employee or group should never take precedence over the goals and well-being of the organization as a whole.
 7. **Remuneration:** Workers must be paid a fair, reasonable salary that rewards their effort equitably.
 8. **Centralization:** An organizational arrangement that decreases the importance of subordinates in decision-making by concentrating authority at the top.
 9. **Scalar Chain:** The distinct line of authority and responsibility running from top management down to the lowest ranks, showing exactly who reports to whom.
 10. **Order:** Ensuring people and materials are in the right place at the right time ("Right man for the right job").
 11. **Equity:** Fair and equitable behavior toward all employees, combining kindness with justice.
 12. **Stability of Tenure of Personnel:** Making employment permanent or stable after observing satisfactory employee performance to ensure efficiency.
 13. **Initiative:** Allowing employees the freedom to originate and carry out plans, which encourages high levels of effort.
 14. **Esprit de Corps:** "Unity is strength." Management must foster a cohesive team spirit and mutual trust among its members.
## 4. Max Weber's Ideal Bureauscacy
Max Weber, a German sociologist, studied organizational activity and described an "ideal type" of structured organization known as a **bureaucracy**.
> **Definition:** A formal organization characterized by a division of labor, a clearly defined hierarchy, detailed rules and regulations, and impersonal relationships.
> 
### Seven Characteristics of a Bureaucratic Organization
 1. **Hierarchy of Authority:** A clear structure where authority flows vertically from top to bottom.
 2. **Division of Work:** Jobs are broken down into simple, routine, and well-defined tasks.
 3. **Career Orientation:** Managers are career professionals who manage the business but do not own the units they operate.
 4. **Formal Rules and Regulations:** A strict system of written rules and standard operating procedures (SOPs) to ensure predictability.
 5. **Formal Selection:** Individuals are hired and promoted based strictly on technical qualifications and merit.
 6. **Role Structure:** A clear functional definition or boundary of workers in a position, requiring detailed job descriptions outlining basic responsibilities and duties.
 7. **Impersonality:** The focus is strictly placed on the position, not the individual. Personal or human considerations are set aside; power belongs to the "office," not the person. (e.g., Addressing an individual simply as "Manager" rather than using their personal name, like "Manager Abdul Karim"). A bureaucracy is a perpetual, continuous entity.
